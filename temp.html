Anthony Weiner, Eliot Spitzer & Liz Cheney: Should They Drop Out?

All have been called on to quit their races

280

Jul 29 by Eileen Shields West

Author of "The World Almanac of Presidential Campaigns"

Elliot Spitzer, Liz Cheney, and Anthony Weiner Politix

Made famous by Kenny Rogers in the late 1970s, the song The Gambler went like this: "You got to know when to hold 'em, know when to fold 'em / Know when to walk away, know when to run." In three high-profile political campaigns - two in New York and one in Wyoming - the celebrity candidate has been asked, urged, or downright told to drop out. For now, all three are staying put, but, as any gambler knows, the odds are not all in their favor.

Take Anthony Weiner. Running for mayor of New York City is never easy, and even less so when you were laid low by a sexting scandal in 2011 and forced to resign from Congress. Weiner asked forgiveness then and said he was ready to begin a new chapter. But what he did not admit then was that his sexting had not stopped. The newly-revealed story of Weiner's continued sexting is now nipping at his heels, forcing his wife, Huma Abedin, to vouch for him. This week's cover of the New Yorker shows Weiner, like King Kong, straddling the Empire State Building with a crazed look and camera in hand. Says artist John Cuneo, "...all I could think about while working on this piece was - will Weiner still be in the race by the time it runs?'" The New York Times and the New York Post have needled him to get out and his campaign manager has just quit. It is clearly time for Weiner to "fold 'em" and walk away.

Eliot Spitzer resigned as governor of New York on March 12, 2008 in disgrace, when it was revealed that he had been "Client 9" in a prostitution ring. Now, after years of being a talking head on television, teaching, and writing columns on how to reform Wall Street, the issue that propelled him to stardom in the first place, Spitzer wants to return to elective office as New York City comptroller. He has also been urged to get out of the race because of his past betrayal of the voters' trust. But there are no new revelations and he has paid a price. Spitzer should "hold 'em" and stay in the race.

Turning West, the biggest primary fight next year may be in the least populated state: Wyoming. Liz Cheney, 46, daughter of former Vice President Dick Cheney, is challenging Mike Enzi, 69, the three-term senator from the Cowboy State, in the 2014 GOP primary. Ever since her announcement, Cheney has been called a carpetbagger, having moved from Virginia to Wyoming last year. She's also accused of using coattails to try to win a race that was once a sure bet for Enzi. But Cheney argues that it is time for a change in Wyoming and in congress. "Things are not getting better; they are getting worse," she says. "It is time to elect a new generation who will step up to the plate on behalf of conservative values." This race will be generational and tea party versus conservative Republican. Neither Cheney nor Enzi should "run" from this one.

Anthony Weiner, Eliot Spitzer & Liz Cheney: Should They Drop Out?

All have been called on to quit their races

280

Jul 29 by Eileen Shields West

Author of "The World Almanac of Presidential Campaigns"

Elliot Spitzer, Liz Cheney, and Anthony Weiner Politix

Made famous by Kenny Rogers in the late 1970s, the song The Gambler went like this: "You got to know when to hold 'em, know when to fold 'em / Know when to walk away, know when to run." In three high-profile political campaigns - two in New York and one in Wyoming - the celebrity candidate has been asked, urged, or downright told to drop out. For now, all three are staying put, but, as any gambler knows, the odds are not all in their favor.

Take Anthony Weiner. Running for mayor of New York City is never easy, and even less so when you were laid low by a sexting scandal in 2011 and forced to resign from Congress. Weiner asked forgiveness then and said he was ready to begin a new chapter. But what he did not admit then was that his sexting had not stopped. The newly-revealed story of Weiner's continued sexting is now nipping at his heels, forcing his wife, Huma Abedin, to vouch for him. This week's cover of the New Yorker shows Weiner, like King Kong, straddling the Empire State Building with a crazed look and camera in hand. Says artist John Cuneo, "...all I could think about while working on this piece was - will Weiner still be in the race by the time it runs?'" The New York Times and the New York Post have needled him to get out and his campaign manager has just quit. It is clearly time for Weiner to "fold 'em" and walk away.

Eliot Spitzer resigned as governor of New York on March 12, 2008 in disgrace, when it was revealed that he had been "Client 9" in a prostitution ring. Now, after years of being a talking head on television, teaching, and writing columns on how to reform Wall Street, the issue that propelled him to stardom in the first place, Spitzer wants to return to elective office as New York City comptroller. He has also been urged to get out of the race because of his past betrayal of the voters' trust. But there are no new revelations and he has paid a price. Spitzer should "hold 'em" and stay in the race.

Turning West, the biggest primary fight next year may be in the least populated state: Wyoming. Liz Cheney, 46, daughter of former Vice President Dick Cheney, is challenging Mike Enzi, 69, the three-term senator from the Cowboy State, in the 2014 GOP primary. Ever since her announcement, Cheney has been called a carpetbagger, having moved from Virginia to Wyoming last year. She's also accused of using coattails to try to win a race that was once a sure bet for Enzi. But Cheney argues that it is time for a change in Wyoming and in congress. "Things are not getting better; they are getting worse," she says. "It is time to elect a new generation who will step up to the plate on behalf of conservative values." This race will be generational and tea party versus conservative Republican. Neither Cheney nor Enzi should "run" from this one.
